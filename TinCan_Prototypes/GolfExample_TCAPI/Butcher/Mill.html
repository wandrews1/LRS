Milling Your Wood

Aquiring Wood


You can purchase hardwoods from a local hardwood mill, at Woodshops like WoodCraft, or even online. Butcher blocks require hardwoods, so be sure not to use softwoods as the knife can slice into the block, leaving deep incisions in your hard work. The deep slices also create a pourous surface known to promote harmful bacteria growth. In this example, I am using a 6 foot, 8/4 (pronounced "Eight-quarter") thick, piece of Black Walnut. 8/4 simply means it is 2 inches thick.

Jointer

Normally, you would use the jointer on one side to aquire a flat side before planing the wood, but I do not have a jointer. I have built a jig that enables me to use my tables saw as a jointer, as seen here. By using a jointer, the wood will now have a perfectly flat face and edge to use in the planer to ensure uniformity.

Planer

Your wood will likely be rough-sawn, meaning the wood has only been sawn with a chainsaw, or equivelant. It is necessary to wear leather gloves while handling because the splinters can be excessive. The wood will need to be ran through a thickness planer for two reasons:

  • Uniform Thickness - The planer makes the wood the same thickness everywhere.
  • Smoothness - The wood will now be smooth enough for barehanded handling.
